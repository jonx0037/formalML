## Overview & Motivation

In the [Graph Laplacians & Spectrum](/topics/graph-laplacians) topic, we studied graphs through the lens of the Laplacian matrix $L = D - A$ and its eigenvalues. The normalized Laplacian $\mathcal{L} = I - D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ made a brief appearance alongside the **transition matrix** $P = D^{-1}A$. Now we place $P$ center stage.

A **random walk** on a graph is the simplest possible stochastic process on a network: at each step, the walker moves to a uniformly random neighbor. Despite this simplicity, the walk's behavior reveals deep structural properties:

- **How quickly does the walk forget its starting position?** The *mixing time* measures convergence to the stationary distribution. Fast mixing implies good connectivity — the graph has no bottlenecks.

- **How far apart are two vertices in "random walk distance"?** The *hitting time* $h(i,j)$ measures the expected number of steps to reach $j$ from $i$. Unlike shortest-path distance, hitting time is sensitive to the graph's branching structure and reflects the "difficulty" of finding a target by random exploration.

- **What is the electrical interpretation?** Identifying edges with unit resistors turns the graph into an electrical network. The *effective resistance* between two vertices equals the commute time (normalized by total edge weight) — a beautiful equivalence between probability and physics.

The spectral gap $\gamma = 1 - \lambda_2(P) = \lambda_2(\mathcal{L})$ is the key quantity: it controls mixing time ($t_{\mathrm{mix}} = \Theta(1/\gamma)$), bounds hitting times, and characterizes **Expander Graphs** *(coming soon)* — sparse graphs that mix as fast as dense ones.

**Roadmap.** We build the theory in layers: define the walk and its transition matrix (§1), prove convergence to the stationary distribution (§2), quantify convergence speed via total variation and mixing time (§3–4), handle bipartite graphs with lazy walks (§5), develop hitting times and commute times (§6–7), and connect everything to applications (§8–9).

## 1. Markov Chains on Graphs

### 1.1 The Transition Matrix

A **random walk** on a graph $G = (V, E)$ with $|V| = n$ is a sequence of random variables $X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots$ taking values in $V$, where at each step the walker moves to a uniformly random neighbor:

$$\Pr[X_{t+1} = j \mid X_t = i] = P_{ij} = \frac{A_{ij}}{d_i}$$

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Random Walk on a Graph">
A **random walk** on a graph $G = (V, E)$ is a Markov chain $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ on the state space $V$ with transition probabilities $P_{ij} = A_{ij} / d_i$: at each step, the walker moves from its current vertex to a uniformly random neighbor.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="Transition Matrix">
The **transition matrix** of the simple random walk on $G$ is:

$$P = D^{-1}A$$

where $D = \mathrm{diag}(d_1, \ldots, d_n)$ is the degree matrix. Each row of $P$ sums to 1 — $P$ is a **(row) stochastic matrix**.
</TheoremBlock>

The entry $P_{ij}$ gives the probability of stepping from vertex $i$ to vertex $j$ in one step. The matrix $P^t$ gives $t$-step transition probabilities: $(P^t)_{ij} = \Pr[X_t = j \mid X_0 = i]$.

![Transition matrices for named graphs — heatmaps showing the row-stochastic structure of P for Path, Cycle, Complete, Star, Barbell, Grid, Hypercube, and Petersen graphs](/images/topics/random-walks/walk-trajectories.png)

### 1.2 Irreducibility and Aperiodicity

Two properties determine the walk's long-run behavior:

- **Irreducible:** Every vertex can be reached from every other vertex (equivalently, $G$ is connected).
- **Aperiodic:** The walk does not get trapped in a deterministic cycle. A connected graph has period 2 if and only if it is **bipartite**. All other connected graphs are aperiodic.

Combining these:

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="Ergodic Chain">
A Markov chain is *ergodic* if it is both irreducible and aperiodic. For graph random walks, this means $G$ is connected and non-bipartite.
</TheoremBlock>

## 2. Stationary Distribution & Convergence

### 2.1 The Stationary Distribution

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={4} title="Stationary Distribution">
A probability distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is *stationary* for $P$ if $\boldsymbol{\pi}^T P = \boldsymbol{\pi}^T$. Equivalently, if the walk's initial distribution is $\boldsymbol{\pi}$, it remains $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ after any number of steps.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Stationarity of π">
The stationary distribution of the simple random walk on $G$ is $\pi_i = d_i / (2m)$ where $m = |E|$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
We verify $\boldsymbol{\pi}^T P = \boldsymbol{\pi}^T$ directly:

$$(\boldsymbol{\pi}^T P)_j = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{d_i}{2m} \cdot \frac{A_{ij}}{d_i} = \frac{1}{2m}\sum_{i=1}^n A_{ij} = \frac{d_j}{2m} = \pi_j$$

The degree in the numerator cancels, and $\sum_i A_{ij} = d_j$ since the column sum of the adjacency matrix equals the degree of vertex $j$.
</TheoremBlock>

The stationary distribution assigns probability proportional to degree. High-degree vertices are visited more often — they are "easier to find" because more edges lead to them. On a $d$-regular graph, all degrees are equal and $\boldsymbol{\pi} = (1/n, \ldots, 1/n)$ — the uniform distribution.

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={1} title="Mixing of Regular Graphs">
On a $d$-regular graph, the stationary distribution is uniform: $\boldsymbol{\pi} = (1/n, \ldots, 1/n)$.
</TheoremBlock>

### 2.2 Detailed Balance (Reversibility)

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Detailed Balance (Reversibility)">
The simple random walk on any undirected graph is reversible: $\pi_i P_{ij} = \pi_j P_{ji}$ for all $i, j$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
$$\pi_i P_{ij} = \frac{d_i}{2m} \cdot \frac{A_{ij}}{d_i} = \frac{A_{ij}}{2m} = \frac{A_{ji}}{2m} = \frac{d_j}{2m} \cdot \frac{A_{ji}}{d_j} = \pi_j P_{ji}$$

The equality $A_{ij} = A_{ji}$ follows from the graph being undirected.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark" title="Reversibility and the Inner Product">
Detailed balance means the walk is *time-reversible*: a video of the walk looks the same played forwards and backwards (in distribution). Algebraically, reversibility is equivalent to $P$ being self-adjoint with respect to the $\ell^2(\boldsymbol{\pi})$ inner product $\langle f, g \rangle_{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \sum_i \pi_i f(i) g(i)$.
</TheoremBlock>

### 2.3 Convergence

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={3} title="Convergence Theorem">
If the random walk on $G$ is ergodic (connected, non-bipartite), then for any starting vertex $x$:

$$\lim_{t \to \infty} P^t(x, \cdot) = \boldsymbol{\pi}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
By the Perron–Frobenius theorem applied to the non-negative irreducible matrix $P$: the largest eigenvalue $\mu_1 = 1$ is simple (by irreducibility), and aperiodicity ensures $|\mu_i| < 1$ for all $i \geq 2$.

The spectral decomposition gives:

$$P^t = \boldsymbol{1}\boldsymbol{\pi}^T + \sum_{i=2}^n \mu_i^t \, \mathbf{f}_i \mathbf{w}_i^T$$

Since $|\mu_i| < 1$, each term $\mu_i^t \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$, so $P^t \to \boldsymbol{1}\boldsymbol{\pi}^T$. That is, every row of $P^t$ converges to $\boldsymbol{\pi}$.
</TheoremBlock>

## 3. Total Variation & Mixing Time

The convergence theorem says the walk eventually reaches stationarity, but *how fast?* We need a distance measure between distributions.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={5} title="Total Variation Distance">
The **total variation distance** between probability distributions $p$ and $q$ on $V$ is:

$$\|p - q\|_{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{v \in V} |p(v) - q(v)|$$
</TheoremBlock>

Total variation ranges from 0 (identical distributions) to 1 (distributions with disjoint support). It has a clean probabilistic interpretation: $\|p - q\|_{\mathrm{TV}} = \max_{A \subseteq V} |p(A) - q(A)|$ — the maximum difference in probability assigned to any event.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={6} title="Mixing Time">
The **mixing time** to accuracy $\varepsilon$ is the worst-case time for the walk to get $\varepsilon$-close to stationarity:

$$t_{\mathrm{mix}}(\varepsilon) = \max_{x \in V} \min\{t \geq 0 : \|P^t(x, \cdot) - \boldsymbol{\pi}\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \leq \varepsilon\}$$

By convention, $t_{\mathrm{mix}} = t_{\mathrm{mix}}(1/4)$.
</TheoremBlock>

**Examples illustrating the range of mixing behaviors:**

- **Complete graph $K_n$:** $t_{\mathrm{mix}} = 1$. Every vertex is adjacent to every other — the walk mixes in a single step.
- **Cycle $C_n$:** $t_{\mathrm{mix}} = \Theta(n^2)$. The walk must diffuse around the ring.
- **Path $P_n$:** $t_{\mathrm{mix}} = \Theta(n^2)$. Similar to the cycle, but without the wraparound.
- **Barbell $B_k$:** $t_{\mathrm{mix}} = \Theta(k^3)$. The bridge is a bottleneck — the walk gets stuck in one clique.
- **Hypercube $Q_d$:** $t_{\mathrm{mix}} = \Theta(d \log d)$. Fast mixing despite sparsity — each vertex has degree $d$ out of $2^d$ vertices.

## 4. Spectral Analysis of Mixing

### 4.1 Spectral Decomposition of $P^t$

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={4} title="Spectral Decomposition of P^t">
Let $\mu_1 = 1 \geq \mu_2 \geq \cdots \geq \mu_n \geq -1$ be the eigenvalues of $P$, with corresponding right eigenvectors $\mathbf{f}_i$ (normalized in $\ell^2(\boldsymbol{\pi})$). Then:

$$P^t(x, y) = \pi_y \left(1 + \sum_{i=2}^n \mu_i^t \frac{f_i(x) f_i(y)}{\|f_i\|_{\pi}^2}\right)$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Since $P$ is similar to the symmetric matrix $S = D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ (via the [Spectral Theorem](/topics/spectral-theorem)), it has real eigenvalues and a complete eigenbasis. Write $P^t$ in the spectral decomposition:

$$P^t = \sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i^t \, \mathbf{f}_i \otimes \mathbf{w}_i$$

where $\mathbf{f}_i$ and $\mathbf{w}_i$ are right and left eigenvectors. The $i = 1$ term is $\boldsymbol{1} \boldsymbol{\pi}^T$ (since $P\boldsymbol{1} = \boldsymbol{1}$ and $\boldsymbol{\pi}^T P = \boldsymbol{\pi}^T$). For ergodic chains, $|\mu_i| < 1$ for $i \geq 2$, so each remaining term decays exponentially.
</TheoremBlock>

The rate of decay is controlled by $\lambda_\star = \max_{i \geq 2} |\mu_i|$, the second-largest eigenvalue *in absolute value*. This leads to:

### 4.2 The Spectral Gap

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={7} title="Spectral Gap">
The **spectral gap** of the random walk is:

$$\gamma = 1 - \lambda_\star \quad \text{where} \quad \lambda_\star = \max_{i \geq 2}|\mu_i|$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={2} title="Spectral Gap Equals λ₂(𝓛)">
For non-bipartite graphs, $\lambda_\star = \mu_2$ and the spectral gap equals the second-smallest eigenvalue of the normalized Laplacian: $\gamma = 1 - \mu_2 = \lambda_2(\mathcal{L})$.
</TheoremBlock>

This is the bridge between random walks and [spectral graph theory](/topics/graph-laplacians): the same eigenvalue $\lambda_2(\mathcal{L})$ that measures algebraic connectivity in the Laplacian world controls mixing speed in the random walk world.

### 4.3 Mixing Time Bounds

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={5} title="Upper Mixing Time Bound">
$$\|P^t(x, \cdot) - \boldsymbol{\pi}\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \leq \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{1/\pi_{\min}} \cdot \lambda_\star^t$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Apply Cauchy–Schwarz in $\ell^2(\boldsymbol{\pi})$. The total variation distance satisfies:

$$\|P^t(x, \cdot) - \boldsymbol{\pi}\|_{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_y \left|\frac{P^t(x,y)}{\pi_y} - 1\right| \pi_y \leq \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\sum_y \left(\frac{P^t(x,y)}{\pi_y} - 1\right)^2 \pi_y}$$

The $\ell^2(\boldsymbol{\pi})$ norm of $P^t(x,\cdot)/\boldsymbol{\pi} - 1$ is bounded by $\sqrt{1/\pi_{\min}} \cdot \lambda_\star^t$ using the spectral decomposition.
</TheoremBlock>

This gives the mixing time bound: $t_{\mathrm{mix}} \leq \frac{1}{\gamma}\left(\frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{1}{\pi_{\min}} + \ln 2\right)$.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={6} title="Lower Mixing Time Bound">
$$t_{\mathrm{mix}}(\varepsilon) \geq \frac{1}{\gamma}\ln\frac{1}{2\varepsilon}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Consider the second eigenvector test function. For the worst-case starting vertex, the TV distance at time $t$ is at least $(1/2)\lambda_\star^t$. Setting this equal to $\varepsilon$ and solving for $t$ gives the lower bound.
</TheoremBlock>

**Summary:** $t_{\mathrm{mix}} = \Theta\left(\frac{1}{\gamma} \ln\frac{1}{\pi_{\min}}\right)$. The spectral gap $\gamma$ is the dominant factor; the $\ln(1/\pi_{\min})$ term accounts for graphs with extreme degree variation.

## 5. Lazy Random Walks

What happens on bipartite graphs? The path graph $P_n$ and the grid graph are bipartite, so $P$ has eigenvalue $\mu_n = -1$ and the walk *oscillates* instead of converging. The distribution at even time steps differs from odd time steps. The fix is simple:

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={8} title="Lazy Random Walk">
The **lazy random walk** has transition matrix:

$$P_{\text{lazy}} = \frac{1}{2}(I + P)$$

At each step, the walker stays put with probability $1/2$ and moves to a random neighbor with probability $1/2$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Lazy Walk Spectrum">
If $\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_n$ are eigenvalues of $P$, then the eigenvalues of $P_{\text{lazy}}$ are $\mu_i^{(\text{lazy})} = (1 + \mu_i)/2$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
If $P\mathbf{v} = \mu_i \mathbf{v}$, then:

$$P_{\text{lazy}} \mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{2}(I + P)\mathbf{v} = \frac{1 + \mu_i}{2}\mathbf{v}$$

So $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of $P_{\text{lazy}}$ with eigenvalue $(1 + \mu_i)/2$.
</TheoremBlock>

Since $\mu_i \in [-1, 1]$, we have $\mu_i^{(\text{lazy})} \in [0, 1]$ — no negative eigenvalues, no oscillation. The lazy walk converges even on bipartite graphs, at the cost of halving the spectral gap: $\gamma_{\text{lazy}} = \gamma / 2$.

## 6. Hitting Times & Commute Times

Mixing time measures convergence of the *distribution* to stationarity. Hitting times measure something complementary: how long it takes a single walk to reach a specific target.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={9} title="Hitting Time">
The **hitting time** from $i$ to $j$ is the expected first passage time:

$$h(i, j) = \mathbb{E}_i[\min\{t \geq 0 : X_t = j\}]$$

with $h(j, j) = 0$ by convention.
</TheoremBlock>

Hitting times satisfy a linear recurrence: $h(i, j) = 1 + \sum_{k \neq j} P_{ik} \, h(k, j)$ for $i \neq j$.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Hitting Time via Fundamental Matrix">
For each target $j$, the hitting times $h(i, j)$ satisfy the linear system:

$$(I - P_{\bar{j}}) \mathbf{h}_j = \mathbf{1}$$

where $P_{\bar{j}}$ is the transition matrix with row and column $j$ removed.
</TheoremBlock>

Hitting times are **asymmetric** in general: $h(i, j) \neq h(j, i)$. On a star graph $S_n$ with 1 hub and $n - 1$ leaves, any leaf reaches the hub in exactly 1 step ($h(\text{leaf}, \text{hub}) = 1$), but the hub must guess the right leaf: $h(\text{hub}, \text{leaf}) = n - 1$. To go from one leaf to another, the walker first steps to the hub (1 step) then searches for the target leaf ($n - 1$ steps on average), giving $h(\text{leaf}_a, \text{leaf}_b) = n$. The asymmetry reflects the graph's branching structure.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={10} title="Commute Time">
The **commute time** between $i$ and $j$ is the expected round-trip time:

$$\kappa(i, j) = h(i, j) + h(j, i)$$

Unlike hitting times, commute times are **symmetric**: $\kappa(i, j) = \kappa(j, i)$.
</TheoremBlock>

## 7. Effective Resistance & Electrical Networks

The connection between random walks and electrical networks is one of the most beautiful results in combinatorics. Replace each edge with a unit resistor, and the graph becomes an electrical circuit. The Laplacian is the **conductance matrix**: applying Kirchhoff's laws gives $L\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{i}_{\text{ext}}$.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={11} title="Effective Resistance">
The **effective resistance** between vertices $i$ and $j$ is:

$$R_{\text{eff}}(i, j) = (\mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j)^T L^\dagger (\mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j)$$

where $L^\dagger$ is the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse of the Laplacian and $\mathbf{e}_i$ is the $i$-th standard basis vector.
</TheoremBlock>

Effective resistance is a **metric** on $V$: it satisfies non-negativity, symmetry, and the triangle inequality. It behaves like physical resistance — adding edges decreases resistance (parallel paths), removing edges increases it.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={7} title="Commute Time–Effective Resistance Identity">
$$\kappa(i, j) = 2m \cdot R_{\text{eff}}(i, j)$$

where $m = |E|$ is the total number of edges.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Express $h(i,j)$ via the fundamental matrix $Z = (I - P + \boldsymbol{1}\boldsymbol{\pi}^T)^{-1}$: we have $h(i,j) = (Z_{jj} - Z_{ij})/\pi_j$. Adding $h(i,j) + h(j,i)$ and using $Z = L^\dagger + (1/n)\boldsymbol{1}\boldsymbol{1}^T$ (after appropriate normalization), the $Z$ entries telescope to give $\kappa(i,j) = 2m(L^\dagger_{ii} + L^\dagger_{jj} - 2L^\dagger_{ij}) = 2m \cdot R_{\text{eff}}(i,j)$.

See Doyle & Snell (1984) for the complete derivation.
</TheoremBlock>

**Properties of effective resistance:**

1. $R_{\text{eff}}(i,j) \geq 0$ with equality if and only if $i = j$.
2. **Symmetric:** $R_{\text{eff}}(i,j) = R_{\text{eff}}(j,i)$.
3. **Triangle inequality:** $R_{\text{eff}}(i,k) \leq R_{\text{eff}}(i,j) + R_{\text{eff}}(j,k)$.
4. **Rayleigh monotonicity:** Adding edges can only decrease effective resistance.

**Random Target Lemma:** $\sum_{i < j} R_{\text{eff}}(i,j) = n \cdot \mathrm{tr}(L^\dagger) = n \sum_{k=2}^n 1/\lambda_k$.

## 8. Applications: PageRank, DeepWalk, Label Propagation

### 8.1 PageRank

PageRank (Page, Brin, Motwani & Winograd, 1999) models a web surfer who follows links randomly, but occasionally "teleports" to a uniformly random page:

$$P_{\text{PR}} = \alpha P + (1 - \alpha) \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T$$

with $\alpha = 0.85$ typically. The teleportation term is a rank-one perturbation that guarantees ergodicity — even if the original graph is disconnected or bipartite.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" title="PageRank as Perturbed Random Walk">
The teleportation term guarantees a spectral gap of at least $1 - \alpha$, so PageRank converges in $O\left(\frac{1}{1 - \alpha} \log n\right)$ iterations regardless of graph structure. This is why power iteration converges fast for PageRank — the teleportation creates an artificial spectral gap.
</TheoremBlock>

![PageRank on a small web graph — node sizes proportional to PageRank score, comparing with the simple random walk stationary distribution](/images/topics/random-walks/pagerank-web-graph.png)

### 8.2 DeepWalk and node2vec

DeepWalk (Perozzi, Al-Rfou & Skiena, 2014) treats random walks as "sentences" and vertices as "words," then applies the skip-gram model from NLP to learn vertex embeddings. The key insight: DeepWalk implicitly factorizes a matrix related to $\log(\max(P^k, 1))$, connecting random walk statistics to low-rank matrix approximations studied in [PCA & Low-Rank Approximation](/topics/pca-low-rank).

### 8.3 Label Propagation

Label propagation spreads known labels through the graph:

$$\mathbf{y}^{(t+1)} = \alpha P \mathbf{y}^{(t)} + (1 - \alpha) \mathbf{y}^{(0)}$$

At convergence, each vertex's label is a discounted average of its neighbors' labels, weighted by the random walk transition probabilities. The mixing time determines how far labels spread — fast-mixing graphs propagate labels globally, while graphs with bottlenecks propagate locally.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" title="Over-Smoothing in GNNs">
Stacking $t$ GCN layers applies $P^t$ to the feature matrix. By the convergence theorem, $P^t \to \boldsymbol{1}\boldsymbol{\pi}^T$ — all vertex features collapse to a single vector. This is **over-smoothing**: the random walk convergence theorem in disguise. The number of useful GNN layers should roughly match $1/\gamma$, the mixing time. **Message Passing & GNNs** *(coming soon)* develops solutions to this problem.
</TheoremBlock>

## 9. Computational Notes

**Transition matrix construction.** Given an adjacency matrix $A$ (as a NumPy array or scipy sparse matrix), the transition matrix is $P = D^{-1}A$:

```python
import numpy as np

def transition_matrix(A):
    """P = D^{-1}A for simple random walk."""
    D_inv = np.diag(1.0 / A.sum(axis=1))
    return D_inv @ A
```

**Random walk simulation.** Simulate a walk and track visit frequencies:

```python
def simulate_walk(P, start, n_steps, rng=None):
    """Simulate a random walk, return visit counts."""
    rng = rng or np.random.default_rng()
    n = len(P)
    counts = np.zeros(n)
    v = start
    for _ in range(n_steps):
        counts[v] += 1
        v = rng.choice(n, p=P[v])
    return counts / n_steps
```

**Mixing time estimation via spectral decomposition.** Compute TV distance at each step without matrix exponentiation:

```python
def mixing_profile(A, max_t=200):
    """TV distance at each step via spectral decomposition."""
    P = transition_matrix(A)
    deg = A.sum(axis=1)
    deg = np.where(deg == 0, 1, deg)  # guard against isolated vertices
    pi = deg / deg.sum()
    inv_sqrt_deg = 1.0 / np.sqrt(deg)
    evals, evecs = np.linalg.eigh(
        np.diag(inv_sqrt_deg) @ A @ np.diag(inv_sqrt_deg)
    )
    # evals of S = D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2} equal evals of P
    tv = []
    for t in range(max_t + 1):
        max_tv = 0
        for x in range(len(A)):
            dist = np.zeros(len(A))
            for k in range(len(A)):
                dist += evals[k]**t * evecs[:, k] * evecs[x, k]
            dist = dist * np.sqrt(A.sum(axis=1)) / np.sqrt(A.sum(axis=1)[x])
            max_tv = max(max_tv, 0.5 * np.sum(np.abs(dist - pi)))
        tv.append(max_tv)
    return tv
```

**Hitting time solver.** Solve the linear system for all-pairs hitting times:

```python
def all_pairs_hitting_times(A):
    """Solve (I - P_{-j}) h_j = 1 for each target j."""
    P = transition_matrix(A)
    n = len(A)
    H = np.zeros((n, n))
    for j in range(n):
        idx = [i for i in range(n) if i != j]
        P_red = P[np.ix_(idx, idx)]
        h = np.linalg.solve(np.eye(n-1) - P_red, np.ones(n-1))
        for k, i in enumerate(idx):
            H[i, j] = h[k]
    return H
```

**Effective resistance.** Compute via the Laplacian pseudoinverse:

```python
def effective_resistance_matrix(A):
    """R_eff(i,j) via Laplacian pseudoinverse."""
    L = np.diag(A.sum(axis=1)) - A
    evals, evecs = np.linalg.eigh(L)
    # Pseudoinverse: invert nonzero eigenvalues
    L_pinv = sum(
        (1/lam) * np.outer(v, v)
        for lam, v in zip(evals, evecs.T) if lam > 1e-10
    )
    R = np.zeros_like(A, dtype=float)
    for i in range(len(A)):
        for j in range(i+1, len(A)):
            R[i,j] = R[j,i] = L_pinv[i,i] + L_pinv[j,j] - 2*L_pinv[i,j]
    return R
```
