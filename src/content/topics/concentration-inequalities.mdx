## Overview & Motivation

The [Measure-Theoretic Probability](/topics/measure-theoretic-probability) topic established that sample averages converge to population means. The Weak Law of Large Numbers tells us $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$. The Strong Law strengthens this to almost sure convergence. But neither law answers the question a practitioner actually asks: **how many samples do we need?**

Suppose we train a classifier on $n$ samples and observe an empirical error rate of 5%. How confident are we that the true error is close to 5%? The law of large numbers says "eventually" — but "eventually" is not a sample size. We need **quantitative bounds on tail probabilities**: explicit formulas relating $n$, the error tolerance $\varepsilon$, and the failure probability $\delta$.

This is exactly what concentration inequalities provide. They answer questions of the form:

$$P\left(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon\right) \leq f(n, \varepsilon)$$

where $f$ decreases (typically exponentially) in $n$ and $\varepsilon$. Inverting these bounds gives us **sample complexity**: the number of samples sufficient to guarantee $|\bar{X}_n - \mu| < \varepsilon$ with probability at least $1 - \delta$.

**The progression of tightness.** We develop a hierarchy of increasingly powerful bounds:

1. **Markov's inequality** uses only the mean — the weakest bound but the most general.
2. **Chebyshev's inequality** adds the variance — a polynomial ($1/n$) rate.
3. **Chernoff bounds** exploit the full moment generating function — exponential ($e^{-cn}$) rates.
4. **Hoeffding's inequality** provides a clean, usable exponential bound for bounded random variables.
5. **Sub-Gaussian theory** abstracts the pattern: a random variable concentrates like a Gaussian if its MGF is controlled by a quadratic.
6. **Bernstein's inequality** refines the bound when the variance is small relative to the range.
7. **McDiarmid's inequality** extends concentration from sums to arbitrary functions of independent variables.

Each step trades generality for tightness. By the end, we will have the precise tools to prove **generalization bounds** — the theoretical guarantee that a machine learning model's training error approximates its true error — and derive **sample complexity** results for finite hypothesis classes.

## Basic Tail Bounds: Markov and Chebyshev

### The Tail Probability Question

Given a random variable $X$ with known moments (mean, variance, etc.), what can we say about the probability that $X$ deviates far from its expected value? This is the **tail probability** question, and it is the foundation of all concentration inequalities.

We begin with the simplest possible bound, which uses only the first moment.

### Markov's Inequality

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Markov's Inequality">
Let $X \geq 0$ be a non-negative random variable with $E[X] < \infty$. Then for any $t > 0$:

$$P(X \geq t) \leq \frac{E[X]}{t}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
We write:

$$E[X] = \int_0^\infty X \, dP \geq \int_{\{X \geq t\}} X \, dP \geq \int_{\{X \geq t\}} t \, dP = t \cdot P(X \geq t)$$

The first inequality follows because $X \geq 0$ (so restricting the domain of integration decreases the integral). The second inequality follows because on the set $\{X \geq t\}$, we have $X \geq t$. Dividing both sides by $t > 0$ completes the proof. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
Markov's inequality is remarkably general — it requires only non-negativity and finite mean. But this generality comes at a cost: the bound is often loose. For example, if $X \sim \text{Exp}(1)$ (so $E[X] = 1$), Markov gives $P(X \geq 5) \leq 1/5 = 0.20$, while the true value is $e^{-5} \approx 0.0067$.
</TheoremBlock>

### Chebyshev's Inequality

We can obtain a tighter bound by exploiting the variance. The idea is simple: apply Markov's inequality to $|X - \mu|^2$.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Chebyshev's Inequality">
Let $X$ have finite mean $\mu = E[X]$ and finite variance $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}(X)$. Then for any $t > 0$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq t) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{t^2}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Since $(X - \mu)^2 \geq 0$, we apply Markov's inequality to $Y = (X - \mu)^2$ with threshold $t^2$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq t) = P((X - \mu)^2 \geq t^2) \leq \frac{E[(X - \mu)^2]}{t^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{t^2} \quad \square$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={1} title="Chebyshev for Sample Means">
If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, then:

$$P\left(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon\right) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
The sample mean $\bar{X}_n$ has mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2/n$. Apply Chebyshev. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
This gives our first **sample complexity** bound: to guarantee $P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) \leq \delta$, we need $n \geq \sigma^2 / (\varepsilon^2 \delta)$. This is the WLLN proof we saw in the previous topic, now made quantitative. But the polynomial rate $O(1/n)$ is far from optimal — we will soon see exponential bounds.
</TheoremBlock>

### One-Sided Variants

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Cantelli's Inequality (One-sided Chebyshev)">
Let $X$ have finite mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Then for any $t > 0$:

$$P(X - \mu \geq t) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + t^2}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
For any $s > 0$, we have:

$$P(X - \mu \geq t) = P(X - \mu + s \geq t + s) \leq \frac{E[(X - \mu + s)^2]}{(t + s)^2} = \frac{\sigma^2 + s^2}{(t+s)^2}$$

by Markov applied to $(X - \mu + s)^2$. Minimizing over $s$ gives $s^* = \sigma^2/t$, yielding the result. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

The figure above demonstrates how loose the basic tail bounds are compared to the true tail probability. In the next section, we introduce the key idea that bridges the gap: the **Chernoff method**.

## The Chernoff Method and Moment Generating Functions

### The Key Idea: Exponentiate and Apply Markov

The breakthrough from polynomial to exponential tail bounds comes from a remarkably simple trick: instead of applying Markov directly to $X$, apply it to $e^{\lambda X}$ for an optimized parameter $\lambda > 0$.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Moment Generating Function">
The *moment generating function* (MGF) of a random variable $X$ is:

$$M_X(\lambda) = E[e^{\lambda X}], \quad \lambda \in \mathbb{R}$$

whenever the expectation exists. The MGF is also called the *Laplace transform* of the distribution of $X$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
The MGF encodes all moments of $X$: $E[X^k] = M_X^{(k)}(0)$. This is why exploiting the MGF gives stronger bounds than using any finite number of moments.
</TheoremBlock>

### The Chernoff Bound

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={3} title="Chernoff Bound">
For any random variable $X$ and any $t \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$P(X \geq t) \leq \inf_{\lambda > 0} e^{-\lambda t} M_X(\lambda)$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
For any $\lambda > 0$, the function $x \mapsto e^{\lambda x}$ is strictly increasing, so:

$$P(X \geq t) = P(e^{\lambda X} \geq e^{\lambda t}) \leq \frac{E[e^{\lambda X}]}{e^{\lambda t}} = e^{-\lambda t} M_X(\lambda)$$

where the inequality is Markov applied to the non-negative random variable $e^{\lambda X}$. Since this holds for all $\lambda > 0$, we take the infimum. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
The optimization over $\lambda$ is the "Chernoff" step. Different choices of $\lambda$ give different trade-offs between the $e^{-\lambda t}$ decay and the MGF growth. The optimal $\lambda^*$ satisfies $M_X'(\lambda^*) / M_X(\lambda^*) = t$, which is the equation for the *Legendre transform* (or *rate function*) of the log-MGF.
</TheoremBlock>

### Application to Sums of Independent Variables

For a sum $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ of independent random variables, the MGF factorizes:

$$M_{S_n}(\lambda) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{X_i}(\lambda)$$

This is because independence gives $E[e^{\lambda S_n}] = E[\prod_i e^{\lambda X_i}] = \prod_i E[e^{\lambda X_i}]$. The log-MGF (cumulant generating function) therefore *adds*:

$$\psi_{S_n}(\lambda) = \sum_{i=1}^n \psi_{X_i}(\lambda)$$

This additivity is why the Chernoff method is particularly powerful for sums of independent variables.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Chernoff for Bernoulli Sums">
Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\text{Ber}(p)$ and $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. For any $t > np$:

$$P(S_n \geq t) \leq \left(\frac{np}{t}\right)^t \left(\frac{n - np}{n - t}\right)^{n-t}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
The MGF of $\text{Ber}(p)$ is $M_{X_i}(\lambda) = 1 - p + pe^{\lambda}$. So $M_{S_n}(\lambda) = (1 - p + pe^{\lambda})^n$. The Chernoff bound gives $P(S_n \geq t) \leq \inf_{\lambda > 0} e^{-\lambda t}(1 - p + pe^{\lambda})^n$. Taking the derivative in $\lambda$ and setting it to zero gives $e^{\lambda^*} = \frac{t(1-p)}{(n-t)p}$. Substituting yields the result after algebraic simplification. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

### The Log-MGF and Cramér's Theorem (Preview)

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="Cumulant Generating Function">
The *cumulant generating function* (CGF, or log-MGF) of $X$ is:

$$\psi_X(\lambda) = \log M_X(\lambda) = \log E[e^{\lambda X}]$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="Rate Function (Legendre–Fenchel Transform)">
The *rate function* of $X$ is:

$$\psi_X^*(t) = \sup_{\lambda \in \mathbb{R}} \left(\lambda t - \psi_X(\lambda)\right)$$

This is the Legendre–Fenchel transform (convex conjugate) of $\psi_X$. The Chernoff bound becomes:

$$P(X \geq t) \leq e^{-\psi_X^*(t)}$$
</TheoremBlock>

The rate function $\psi_X^*(t)$ measures how "surprising" it is to observe $X \geq t$. This connects to large deviations theory and information-theoretic quantities like the KL divergence.

![The Chernoff method — optimizing the exponential tilt parameter λ, comparing Chernoff vs Chebyshev bounds for a Binomial distribution, and the rate function (Legendre transform)](/images/topics/concentration-inequalities/chernoff-method.png)

```python
# Chernoff Method: Optimizing the Exponential Tilt
p, n = 0.3, 100
t_target = 40  # P(S_n >= 40) where np = 30

lambdas = np.linspace(0.01, 2.0, 200)
chernoff_bounds = []
for lam in lambdas:
    mgf = (1 - p + p * np.exp(lam))**n
    bound = np.exp(-lam * t_target) * mgf
    chernoff_bounds.append(bound)

chernoff_bounds = np.array(chernoff_bounds)
opt_idx = np.argmin(chernoff_bounds)

# The optimal λ* minimizes the Chernoff bound
print(f"Optimal λ = {lambdas[opt_idx]:.3f}")
print(f"Chernoff bound = {chernoff_bounds[opt_idx]:.2e}")
print(f"True probability = {1 - stats.binom.cdf(39, n, p):.2e}")
```

## Hoeffding's Inequality

The Chernoff method is powerful but requires computing the MGF of each distribution. Hoeffding's inequality provides a **distribution-free** exponential bound for bounded random variables, avoiding the need to compute the specific MGF.

### Hoeffding's Lemma

The key technical ingredient is a bound on the MGF of a bounded, zero-mean random variable.

<TheoremBlock type="lemma" number={1} title="Hoeffding's Lemma">
Let $X$ be a random variable with $E[X] = 0$ and $a \leq X \leq b$ almost surely. Then for all $\lambda > 0$:

$$E[e^{\lambda X}] \leq \exp\left(\frac{\lambda^2 (b - a)^2}{8}\right)$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Since $a \leq X \leq b$, we can write $X = \alpha b + (1 - \alpha) a$ where $\alpha = \frac{X - a}{b - a} \in [0, 1]$. By convexity of $e^{\lambda x}$:

$$e^{\lambda X} \leq \frac{X - a}{b - a} e^{\lambda b} + \frac{b - X}{b - a} e^{\lambda a}$$

Taking expectations and using $E[X] = 0$ (so $E[X - a] = -a$ and $E[b - X] = b$):

$$E[e^{\lambda X}] \leq \frac{-a}{b - a} e^{\lambda b} + \frac{b}{b - a} e^{\lambda a}$$

Let $h = \lambda(b - a)$, $p = \frac{-a}{b - a}$. Then the right side equals $e^{g(h)}$ where:

$$g(h) = -ph + \log(1 - p + pe^h)$$

We have $g(0) = 0$, $g'(0) = 0$, and $g''(h) \leq 1/4$ for all $h$ (since $g''(h) = \frac{pe^h(1-p+pe^h) - p^2 e^{2h}}{(1-p+pe^h)^2}$ is maximized at $p = 1/2$). By Taylor's theorem with the mean-value form of the remainder:

$$g(h) \leq \frac{h^2}{8} = \frac{\lambda^2(b-a)^2}{8} \quad \square$$
</TheoremBlock>

### Hoeffding's Inequality

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={4} title="Hoeffding's Inequality">
Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent random variables with $a_i \leq X_i \leq b_i$ almost surely. Let $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. Then for any $t > 0$:

$$P(S_n - E[S_n] \geq t) \leq \exp\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right)$$

and by a symmetric argument:

$$P(|S_n - E[S_n]| \geq t) \leq 2\exp\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right)$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Apply the Chernoff method: for any $\lambda > 0$, by independence:

$$E\left[e^{\lambda(S_n - E[S_n])}\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n E\left[e^{\lambda(X_i - E[X_i])}\right]$$

Each $Y_i = X_i - E[X_i]$ satisfies $E[Y_i] = 0$ and $a_i - E[X_i] \leq Y_i \leq b_i - E[X_i]$, with range $b_i - a_i$. By Hoeffding's Lemma:

$$E\left[e^{\lambda Y_i}\right] \leq \exp\left(\frac{\lambda^2(b_i - a_i)^2}{8}\right)$$

So:

$$P(S_n - E[S_n] \geq t) \leq \exp\left(-\lambda t + \frac{\lambda^2}{8}\sum_{i=1}^n(b_i - a_i)^2\right)$$

Minimizing over $\lambda$ gives $\lambda^* = \frac{4t}{\sum_i (b_i - a_i)^2}$, yielding:

$$P(S_n - E[S_n] \geq t) \leq \exp\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right) \quad \square$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={2} title="Hoeffding for Sample Means">
If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d. with $a \leq X_i \leq b$, then:

$$P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) \leq 2\exp\left(-\frac{2n\varepsilon^2}{(b-a)^2}\right)$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
Inverting: to achieve $P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) \leq \delta$, we need:

$$n \geq \frac{(b-a)^2}{2\varepsilon^2}\log\frac{2}{\delta}$$

Compare to Chebyshev's $n \geq \sigma^2/(\varepsilon^2\delta)$: Hoeffding's dependence on $\delta$ is $\log(1/\delta)$ instead of $1/\delta$ — exponentially better for small $\delta$.
</TheoremBlock>

![Hoeffding's inequality — empirical verification showing the bound vs Monte Carlo estimates, sample complexity comparison, and tightness across different distributions](/images/topics/concentration-inequalities/hoeffding-inequality.png)

```python
# Hoeffding's Inequality: Empirical Verification
np.random.seed(42)
n_values = np.arange(10, 501, 5)
eps = 0.2
a, b = 0, 1  # Uniform[0,1]
mu, sigma2 = 0.5, 1/12
n_mc = 50000

empirical_probs = []
for n in n_values:
    samples = np.random.uniform(0, 1, (n_mc, n))
    means = samples.mean(axis=1)
    empirical_probs.append(np.mean(np.abs(means - mu) >= eps))

hoeffding_bound = 2 * np.exp(-2 * n_values * eps**2 / (b - a)**2)
chebyshev_bound = np.minimum(sigma2 / (n_values * eps**2), 1.0)

# The Hoeffding bound is a valid upper bound at every n
# Chebyshev is always looser (higher) than Hoeffding
```

## Sub-Gaussian Random Variables

Hoeffding's inequality works for bounded random variables, but many important random variables are unbounded (e.g., Gaussian). We need a framework that captures the *tail behavior* rather than relying on boundedness. This leads to the theory of **sub-Gaussian** random variables — a class defined by having tails that decay at least as fast as a Gaussian's.

### Motivation

A standard Gaussian $Z \sim N(0, 1)$ has MGF $E[e^{\lambda Z}] = e^{\lambda^2/2}$. The Chernoff method gives:

$$P(Z \geq t) \leq e^{-t^2/2}$$

This Gaussian tail bound has the form $e^{-ct^2}$. Many distributions — even non-Gaussian ones — exhibit this same qualitative behavior. Sub-Gaussian theory captures this commonality.

### Definition and Equivalent Characterizations

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={4} title="Sub-Gaussian Random Variable">
A random variable $X$ with $E[X] = 0$ is *sub-Gaussian with parameter $\sigma$* (written $X \sim \text{subG}(\sigma^2)$) if its MGF satisfies:

$$E[e^{\lambda X}] \leq e^{\sigma^2 \lambda^2 / 2} \quad \text{for all } \lambda \in \mathbb{R}$$

The parameter $\sigma$ is called the *sub-Gaussian proxy variance* (or sub-Gaussian norm).
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={3} title="Equivalent Characterizations of Sub-Gaussian">
For a mean-zero random variable $X$, the following are equivalent (up to universal constants in the parameters):

1. **MGF condition:** $E[e^{\lambda X}] \leq e^{\sigma^2 \lambda^2 / 2}$ for all $\lambda$.
2. **Tail condition:** $P(|X| \geq t) \leq 2e^{-t^2/(2\sigma^2)}$ for all $t \geq 0$.
3. **Moment condition:** $\|X\|_p = (E[|X|^p])^{1/p} \leq \sigma\sqrt{p}$ for all $p \geq 1$.
4. **$\psi_2$-norm:** $\|X\|_{\psi_2} = \inf\{t > 0 : E[e^{X^2/t^2}] \leq 2\} < \infty$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
These characterizations are not equivalent with the *same* constant — they are equivalent up to universal multiplicative factors. The MGF condition is the cleanest for proving concentration bounds. The tail condition is most intuitive.
</TheoremBlock>

### Key Examples

**Example 1 (Gaussian).** $Z \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$ is $\text{subG}(\sigma^2)$ — the defining example.

**Example 2 (Rademacher).** $X = \pm 1$ with equal probability. Then $E[e^{\lambda X}] = \cosh(\lambda) \leq e^{\lambda^2/2}$, so $X \sim \text{subG}(1)$.

**Example 3 (Bounded).** If $a \leq X \leq b$ and $E[X] = 0$, then $X \sim \text{subG}((b-a)^2/4)$ by Hoeffding's Lemma.

**Example 4 (Not sub-Gaussian).** $X \sim \text{Exp}(1) - 1$ is *not* sub-Gaussian: its MGF $E[e^{\lambda X}] = e^{-\lambda}/(1 - \lambda)$ diverges at $\lambda = 1$, so it cannot be bounded by a quadratic for all $\lambda$.

### Concentration for Sums of Sub-Gaussians

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={5} title="Sub-Gaussian Sum">
If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with $X_i \sim \text{subG}(\sigma_i^2)$, then $\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \sim \text{subG}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2\right)$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
By independence:

$$E\left[\exp\left(\lambda \sum_i X_i\right)\right] = \prod_i E[e^{\lambda X_i}] \leq \prod_i e^{\sigma_i^2 \lambda^2/2} = \exp\left(\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\sum_i \sigma_i^2\right) \quad \square$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={3} title="Sub-Gaussian Sample Means">
If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d. $\text{subG}(\sigma^2)$, then:

$$P\left(|\bar{X}_n| \geq \varepsilon\right) \leq 2\exp\left(-\frac{n\varepsilon^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$
</TheoremBlock>

### Maximal Inequality

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={6} title="Sub-Gaussian Maximum">
If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are (not necessarily independent) sub-Gaussian with parameter $\sigma^2$, then:

$$E\left[\max_{1 \leq i \leq n} X_i\right] \leq \sigma\sqrt{2\log n}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
For any $\lambda > 0$:

$$E\left[\max_i X_i\right] \leq \frac{1}{\lambda}\log E\left[e^{\lambda \max_i X_i}\right] \leq \frac{1}{\lambda}\log\left(\sum_{i=1}^n E[e^{\lambda X_i}]\right) \leq \frac{1}{\lambda}\log(ne^{\sigma^2\lambda^2/2}) = \frac{\log n}{\lambda} + \frac{\sigma^2\lambda}{2}$$

where we used $\max_i X_i \leq \frac{1}{\lambda}\log\sum_i e^{\lambda X_i}$ (log-sum-exp) and Jensen's inequality. Optimizing: $\lambda^* = \sqrt{2\log n}/\sigma$ gives the result. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
The $\sqrt{\log n}$ rate is ubiquitous in high-dimensional statistics. It tells us that the maximum of $n$ sub-Gaussian variables grows only logarithmically — a fundamental reason why many statistical procedures work in high dimensions.
</TheoremBlock>

## Sub-Exponential Random Variables and Bernstein's Inequality

### Beyond Sub-Gaussian: Heavier Tails

Not all useful random variables are sub-Gaussian. The centered exponential $X - E[X]$ where $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, or the square $Z^2$ of a Gaussian, have tails that decay as $e^{-ct}$ rather than $e^{-ct^2}$. These are **sub-exponential** random variables.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={5} title="Sub-Exponential Random Variable">
A random variable $X$ with $E[X] = 0$ is *sub-exponential with parameters $(\nu^2, b)$* if:

$$E[e^{\lambda X}] \leq \exp\left(\frac{\nu^2 \lambda^2}{2}\right) \quad \text{for all } |\lambda| < \frac{1}{b}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
The MGF condition looks like the sub-Gaussian condition, but it only holds for $|\lambda| < 1/b$ rather than all $\lambda$. This restricted domain reflects the heavier tails.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={4} title="Sub-Gaussian ⊂ Sub-Exponential">
Every sub-Gaussian random variable is sub-exponential (with $b = 0$, formally). The converse is false: $Z^2 - 1$ where $Z \sim N(0,1)$ is sub-exponential but not sub-Gaussian.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={5} title="Product Rule (Sub-Gaussian squares)">
If $X \sim \text{subG}(\sigma^2)$, then $X^2 - E[X^2]$ is sub-exponential with parameters $(\nu^2, b)$ depending on $\sigma$.
</TheoremBlock>

### Bernstein's Inequality

Bernstein's inequality is sharper than Hoeffding when the variance is small relative to the range. It interpolates between sub-Gaussian behavior (for small deviations) and sub-exponential behavior (for large deviations).

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={7} title="Bernstein's Inequality">
Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent mean-zero random variables with $|X_i| \leq M$ almost surely. Let $\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i^2]$. Then:

$$P\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \geq t\right) \leq \exp\left(-\frac{n t^2 / 2}{\sigma^2 + Mt/3}\right)$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
For bounded $X_i$ with $|X_i| \leq M$, we have $E[|X_i|^k] \leq \frac{k!}{2} \sigma_i^2 M^{k-2}$ for $k \geq 2$ (Bernstein's moment condition). This gives:

$$E[e^{\lambda X_i}] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{\lambda^k E[X_i^k]}{k!} \leq 1 + \frac{\lambda^2\sigma_i^2}{2}\sum_{k=0}^\infty (M|\lambda|)^k = 1 + \frac{\lambda^2\sigma_i^2/2}{1 - M|\lambda|}$$

for $|\lambda| < 1/M$. Using $1 + x \leq e^x$ and the Chernoff method, then optimizing $\lambda$, we obtain the result. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
The denominator $\sigma^2 + Mt/3$ creates two regimes:
- **Small deviations** ($t \ll \sigma^2/M$): the bound behaves as $\exp(-nt^2/(2\sigma^2))$ — sub-Gaussian with the *variance*, not the range.
- **Large deviations** ($t \gg \sigma^2/M$): the bound behaves as $\exp(-nt/(2M/3))$ — sub-exponential, linear in $t$.

When $\sigma^2 \ll (b-a)^2$, Bernstein is much tighter than Hoeffding in the small-deviation regime.
</TheoremBlock>

## McDiarmid's Inequality

### From Sums to General Functions

Hoeffding's inequality applies to sums $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i X_i$. But in machine learning, we often care about more general functions of independent random variables — for instance, the empirical risk $\hat{R}(h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(h(X_i), Y_i)$, or the supremum of an empirical process $\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} |\hat{R}(h) - R(h)|$.

McDiarmid's inequality extends concentration to *any* function $f(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ that doesn't depend too much on any single coordinate.

### The Bounded Differences Condition

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={6} title="Bounded Differences Property">
A function $f : \mathcal{X}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfies the *bounded differences property* with constants $c_1, \ldots, c_n$ if for every $i$ and every $x_1, \ldots, x_n, x_i' \in \mathcal{X}$:

$$\sup_{x_1, \ldots, x_n, x_i'} |f(x_1, \ldots, x_i, \ldots, x_n) - f(x_1, \ldots, x_i', \ldots, x_n)| \leq c_i$$

In words: changing any single input $x_i$ to $x_i'$ changes $f$ by at most $c_i$.
</TheoremBlock>

### McDiarmid's Inequality

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={8} title="McDiarmid's Inequality">
Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent random variables, and let $f$ satisfy the bounded differences property with constants $c_1, \ldots, c_n$. Then:

$$P(f(X_1, \ldots, X_n) - E[f(X_1, \ldots, X_n)] \geq t) \leq \exp\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n c_i^2}\right)$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
The proof uses a **martingale difference** decomposition. Define:

$$D_i = E[f \mid X_1, \ldots, X_i] - E[f \mid X_1, \ldots, X_{i-1}]$$

Then $f - E[f] = \sum_{i=1}^n D_i$ is a telescoping sum of martingale differences ($E[D_i \mid X_1, \ldots, X_{i-1}] = 0$).

The bounded differences condition implies $|D_i| \leq c_i$ almost surely. Each $D_i$ is conditionally bounded and mean-zero given $X_1, \ldots, X_{i-1}$, so Hoeffding's Lemma applies conditionally:

$$E[e^{\lambda D_i} \mid X_1, \ldots, X_{i-1}] \leq e^{\lambda^2 c_i^2 / 8}$$

The remainder follows the same Chernoff optimization as Hoeffding's inequality, yielding the stated bound with constant $2$ instead of $8$ by tighter analysis of the conditional ranges. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
McDiarmid's inequality is sometimes called the "method of bounded differences." It subsumes Hoeffding's inequality: for $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i$ with $x_i \in [a, b]$, each $c_i = (b-a)/n$, and the bound reduces to Hoeffding.
</TheoremBlock>

### Application: Concentration of Empirical Risk

**Example 5 (Empirical Risk).** Let $(X_1, Y_1), \ldots, (X_n, Y_n)$ be i.i.d. training samples, $h$ a fixed hypothesis, and $\ell$ a loss function bounded by $M$. Define the empirical risk:

$$\hat{R}(h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(h(X_i), Y_i)$$

Changing any single sample $(X_i, Y_i)$ changes $\hat{R}(h)$ by at most $M/n$. By McDiarmid:

$$P(|\hat{R}(h) - R(h)| \geq \varepsilon) \leq 2\exp(-2n\varepsilon^2/M^2)$$

This is the concentration result that enables **generalization bounds**: the empirical risk concentrates around the true risk at an exponential rate.

## Applications to Machine Learning

### Generalization Bounds for a Fixed Hypothesis

The simplest generalization bound follows directly from Hoeffding's inequality (or McDiarmid). Given a hypothesis $h$, its true risk $R(h) = E[\ell(h(X), Y)]$ and empirical risk $\hat{R}(h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(h(X_i), Y_i)$, if the loss is bounded by $M$:

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={9} title="Fixed-Hypothesis Generalization Bound">
With probability at least $1 - \delta$:

$$|R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \leq M\sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\delta)}{2n}}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Apply Hoeffding's Corollary 2 with $\varepsilon = M\sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\delta)}{2n}}$ and solve $2e^{-2n\varepsilon^2/M^2} = \delta$. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

### The Union Bound and Finite Hypothesis Classes

For a finite hypothesis class $\mathcal{H} = \{h_1, \ldots, h_K\}$, we want uniform convergence: $\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} |\hat{R}(h) - R(h)| \leq \varepsilon$ simultaneously for all $h$. The **union bound** (Boole's inequality) and Hoeffding give:

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={10} title="Uniform Convergence for Finite H">
With probability at least $1 - \delta$:

$$\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \leq M\sqrt{\frac{\log(2|\mathcal{H}|/\delta)}{2n}}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
By the union bound:

$$P(\exists h : |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \geq \varepsilon) \leq \sum_{k=1}^K P(|\hat{R}(h_k) - R(h_k)| \geq \varepsilon) \leq 2Ke^{-2n\varepsilon^2/M^2}$$

Set this equal to $\delta$ and solve for $\varepsilon$. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
The price of uniformity is $\log|\mathcal{H}|$ — logarithmic in the size of the hypothesis class. This is remarkably mild: even with $|\mathcal{H}| = 10^{100}$ hypotheses, we only need $\log(10^{100}) \approx 230$ additional samples' worth of concentration.
</TheoremBlock>

### Sample Complexity

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={4} title="Sample Complexity for Finite H">
To achieve $\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \leq \varepsilon$ with probability $\geq 1 - \delta$, it suffices to have:

$$n \geq \frac{M^2}{2\varepsilon^2}\log\frac{2|\mathcal{H}|}{\delta}$$
</TheoremBlock>

This is the **PAC-style sample complexity** bound. The dependence on the key parameters is:
- **$1/\varepsilon^2$**: quadratic in the accuracy — fundamental to most concentration-based bounds.
- **$\log|\mathcal{H}|$**: logarithmic in the hypothesis class size — the key insight that makes learning tractable.
- **$\log(1/\delta)$**: logarithmic in the confidence — exponentially better than Chebyshev's $1/\delta$.

### The Bridge to PAC Learning

This section has developed the concentration machinery that the [PAC Learning Framework](/topics/pac-learning) will use extensively. The key ingredients flowing forward are:

1. **Hoeffding's inequality** for bounding the gap between empirical and true risk.
2. **The union bound** for extending single-hypothesis bounds to finite classes.
3. **Sub-Gaussian theory** as the abstract framework for tail control.
4. **McDiarmid's inequality** for concentration of general functions (needed for Rademacher complexity).

The PAC learning framework formalizes this: a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ is *PAC-learnable* if there exists an algorithm that, given $n \geq n(\varepsilon, \delta, \mathcal{H})$ i.i.d. samples, outputs a hypothesis $\hat{h}$ with $R(\hat{h}) - \inf_{h \in \mathcal{H}} R(h) \leq \varepsilon$ with probability $\geq 1 - \delta$. The sample complexity $n(\varepsilon, \delta, \mathcal{H})$ depends on the "complexity" of $\mathcal{H}$ — measured by VC dimension, Rademacher complexity, or covering numbers — all of which rely on the concentration inequalities developed here.

# Generalization Bounds: Union Bound + Hoeffding for Finite H
np.random.seed(42)
n_vals = np.arange(50, 2001, 20)
true_risk = 0.3
n_mc = 5000

# Empirical generalization gaps (Monte Carlo)
gen_gaps_95 = []
for n in n_vals:
    emp_risks = np.random.binomial(n, true_risk, n_mc) / n
    gaps = np.abs(emp_risks - true_risk)
    gen_gaps_95.append(np.percentile(gaps, 95))

# Hoeffding bound for 95% confidence (delta = 0.05)
hoeffding_95 = np.sqrt(np.log(2 / 0.05) / (2 * n_vals))

# Sample complexity: n >= log(2|H|/delta) / (2 * eps^2)
# For |H| = 100, delta = 0.05:
n_needed = lambda eps: np.log(2 * 100 / 0.05) / (2 * eps**2)
