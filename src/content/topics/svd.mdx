## Overview & Motivation

The [Spectral Theorem](/topics/spectral-theorem) told us that every symmetric matrix decomposes into orthogonal eigenvectors and real eigenvalues. That's powerful — but it only works for square, symmetric matrices. Most matrices we meet in practice are neither.

A data matrix with $n$ observations in $d$ features is $n \times d$. A term-document matrix in NLP is $|\text{vocab}| \times |\text{docs}|$. An image is $\text{rows} \times \text{cols}$. None of these are square. None are symmetric. The Spectral Theorem says nothing about them directly.

The **Singular Value Decomposition** fills this gap. Every matrix — any shape, any rank — has an SVD:

$$A = U \Sigma V^T$$

where $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal and $\Sigma$ is diagonal with non-negative entries. The diagonal entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ are the **singular values**, and they measure how much the matrix stretches space in each direction. The columns of $V$ are the input directions, and the columns of $U$ are the output directions.

This decomposition is the single most useful factorization in applied mathematics. It powers:

- **PCA**: the principal components of a centered data matrix $X$ are exactly the right singular vectors of $X$.
- **Low-rank approximation**: the truncated SVD $A_k$ is the *best* rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ (Eckart–Young theorem).
- **The pseudoinverse**: $A^+ = V \Sigma^+ U^T$ solves least-squares problems.
- **Image compression**: keeping only the top $k$ singular values compresses an image with provably minimal error.
- **Latent Semantic Analysis**: SVD on a term-document matrix discovers hidden topic dimensions.
- **Condition numbers**: $\kappa(A) = \sigma_1 / \sigma_r$ measures numerical sensitivity.

## From the Spectral Theorem to the SVD

### The Key Insight: $A^T A$ and $A A^T$

Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ be any matrix (not necessarily square or symmetric). Consider the two products:

- $A^T A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ — symmetric and PSD, since $x^T (A^T A) x = \|Ax\|^2 \geq 0$.
- $A A^T \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ — symmetric and PSD, since $y^T (A A^T) y = \|A^T y\|^2 \geq 0$.

By the [Spectral Theorem](/topics/spectral-theorem), both $A^T A$ and $A A^T$ have orthogonal eigendecompositions with real non-negative eigenvalues. The SVD arises from the observation that **these two eigendecompositions are intimately linked**.

### Shared Eigenvalues

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Shared eigenvalues of $A^T A$ and $A A^T$">
$A^T A$ and $A A^T$ have the same nonzero eigenvalues.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Suppose $A^T A v = \lambda v$ with $\lambda \neq 0$. Set $u = Av / \|Av\|$. Then:

$$A A^T u = A A^T \frac{Av}{\|Av\|} = \frac{A(A^T A v)}{\|Av\|} = \frac{A(\lambda v)}{\|Av\|} = \lambda \frac{Av}{\|Av\|} = \lambda u$$

So $u$ is an eigenvector of $A A^T$ with the same eigenvalue $\lambda$. The converse direction is symmetric: if $A A^T u = \lambda u$ with $\lambda \neq 0$, then $v = A^T u / \|A^T u\|$ satisfies $A^T A v = \lambda v$.
</TheoremBlock>

### Singular Values

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Singular values and singular vectors">
The **singular values** of $A$ are the square roots of the nonzero eigenvalues of $A^T A$ (equivalently, of $A A^T$):

$$\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^T A)}$$

ordered as $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ where $r = \text{rank}(A)$. The corresponding eigenvectors of $A^T A$ are the **right singular vectors** $v_i$, and the eigenvectors of $A A^T$ are the **left singular vectors** $u_i$. They satisfy $A v_i = \sigma_i u_i$.
</TheoremBlock>

## The SVD: Statement and Proof

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Singular Value Decomposition">
Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with $\text{rank}(A) = r$. Then there exist:

- An orthogonal matrix $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ (columns are left singular vectors),
- An orthogonal matrix $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ (columns are right singular vectors),
- A diagonal matrix $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ on the diagonal and zeros elsewhere,

such that $A = U \Sigma V^T$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
**Via the Spectral Theorem applied to $A^T A$.**

**Step 1.** $A^T A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is symmetric and PSD (we showed this above). By the [Spectral Theorem](/topics/spectral-theorem), there exists an orthogonal matrix $V = [v_1 \mid \cdots \mid v_n]$ and non-negative eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n \geq 0$ such that $A^T A = V \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n) V^T$. Exactly $r$ of these eigenvalues are positive (since $\text{rank}(A^T A) = \text{rank}(A) = r$). Set $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$ for $i = 1, \ldots, r$.

**Step 2.** For $i = 1, \ldots, r$, define $u_i = A v_i / \sigma_i$. We verify that $\{u_1, \ldots, u_r\}$ is orthonormal:

$$u_i^T u_j = \frac{(A v_i)^T (A v_j)}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = \frac{v_i^T (A^T A) v_j}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = \frac{v_i^T (\lambda_j v_j)}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = \frac{\lambda_j \, v_i^T v_j}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = \frac{\lambda_j \, \delta_{ij}}{\sigma_i \sigma_j}$$

For $i = j$: $u_i^T u_i = \lambda_i / \sigma_i^2 = 1$. For $i \neq j$: $u_i^T u_j = 0$.

**Step 3.** Extend $\{u_1, \ldots, u_r\}$ to an orthonormal basis $\{u_1, \ldots, u_m\}$ of $\mathbb{R}^m$ (Gram–Schmidt on any complementary vectors). Set $U = [u_1 \mid \cdots \mid u_m]$.

**Step 4.** We have $A v_i = \sigma_i u_i$ for $i \leq r$ and $A v_i = 0$ for $i > r$ (since $v_i \in \ker(A)$ when $\lambda_i = 0$). In matrix form, this gives $AV = U \Sigma$, hence $A = U \Sigma V^T$.
</TheoremBlock>

### Forms of the SVD

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="Forms of the SVD">
| Form | $U$ | $\Sigma$ | $V^T$ | Size |
|------|-----|----------|-------|------|
| **Full** | $m \times m$ | $m \times n$ | $n \times n$ | All singular vectors |
| **Thin** | $m \times k$ | $k \times k$ | $k \times n$ | $k = \min(m,n)$ singular vectors |
| **Compact** | $m \times r$ | $r \times r$ | $r \times n$ | Only $r = \text{rank}(A)$ singular vectors |
| **Truncated** | $m \times k$ | $k \times k$ | $k \times n$ | Only the top $k$ singular vectors |
</TheoremBlock>

The **outer product form** expands the compact SVD as a sum of rank-1 matrices:

$$A = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i \, u_i v_i^T$$

Each term $\sigma_i \, u_i v_i^T$ is a rank-1 matrix, weighted by the singular value $\sigma_i$. This is the representation that makes the Eckart–Young theorem transparent.

```python
import numpy as np

A = np.array([
    [1, 2, 0],
    [0, 1, 3],
    [2, 0, 1],
    [1, 1, 1]
], dtype=float)

# Compute the thin SVD
U, sigma, Vt = np.linalg.svd(A, full_matrices=False)
print(f"U shape: {U.shape}")   # (4, 3) — thin
print(f"Σ: {sigma.round(4)}")  # [3.8826, 2.2465, 1.6966]
print(f"Vt shape: {Vt.shape}") # (3, 3)

# Verify: A = U @ diag(sigma) @ Vt
A_reconstructed = U @ np.diag(sigma) @ Vt
print(f"||A - UΣVᵀ||_F = {np.linalg.norm(A - A_reconstructed):.2e}")

# Outer product form: A = sum sigma_i * u_i * v_i^T
A_outer = sum(sigma[i] * np.outer(U[:, i], Vt[i, :])
              for i in range(len(sigma)))
print(f"||A - Σ σᵢ uᵢvᵢᵀ||_F = {np.linalg.norm(A - A_outer):.2e}")
```

## Geometric Interpretation

### Rotate–Stretch–Rotate

The SVD tells us that **every linear map is a rotation, followed by an axis-aligned stretch, followed by another rotation**. This is the geometric content of $A = U \Sigma V^T$:

1. **$V^T$** rotates the input space to align the right singular vectors with the coordinate axes.
2. **$\Sigma$** stretches each axis by the corresponding singular value $\sigma_i$.
3. **$U$** rotates the result to align with the left singular vectors in the output space.

### The Four Fundamental Subspaces

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="The four fundamental subspaces">
The SVD reveals all four fundamental subspaces of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with rank $r$:

| Subspace | Symbol | Basis from SVD | Dimension |
|----------|--------|---------------|-----------|
| Column space (range) | $\mathcal{C}(A)$ | $u_1, \ldots, u_r$ | $r$ |
| Left null space | $\mathcal{N}(A^T)$ | $u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_m$ | $m - r$ |
| Row space | $\mathcal{C}(A^T)$ | $v_1, \ldots, v_r$ | $r$ |
| Null space | $\mathcal{N}(A)$ | $v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n$ | $n - r$ |

These four subspaces satisfy $\mathcal{C}(A) \perp \mathcal{N}(A^T)$ and $\mathcal{C}(A^T) \perp \mathcal{N}(A)$.
</TheoremBlock>

### The Pseudoinverse

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={4} title="Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse">
For $A = U \Sigma V^T$ with rank $r$, the **pseudoinverse** is:

$$A^+ = V \Sigma^+ U^T = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \frac{1}{\sigma_i} v_i u_i^T$$

where $\Sigma^+$ is obtained by transposing $\Sigma$ and inverting the nonzero diagonal entries. $A^+$ satisfies $A^+ A = V_r V_r^T$ (projection onto the row space) and $A A^+ = U_r U_r^T$ (projection onto the column space). For the least-squares problem $\min_x \|Ax - b\|_2$, the minimum-norm solution is $x^* = A^+ b$.
</TheoremBlock>

## Low-Rank Approximation

### The Truncated SVD

Given the outer product form $A = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i \, u_i v_i^T$, we can approximate $A$ by keeping only the first $k$ terms:

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i \, u_i v_i^T = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^T$$

This is the **rank-$k$ truncated SVD**. It throws away the smallest $r - k$ singular values and their associated directions. The question is: how good is this approximation?

The answer is: **optimal**.

### The Eckart–Young–Mirsky Theorem

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Eckart–Young–Mirsky Theorem">
Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with singular values $\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$. For any $k \leq r$, the rank-$k$ truncated SVD $A_k$ is the best rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ in both the Frobenius and spectral norms:

$$\min_{\text{rank}(B) \leq k} \|A - B\|_F = \|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2}$$

$$\min_{\text{rank}(B) \leq k} \|A - B\|_2 = \|A - A_k\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
**Frobenius norm.** We use three facts: (1) the Frobenius norm is unitarily invariant, $\|UAV^T\|_F = \|A\|_F$; (2) the Frobenius norm of a diagonal matrix is the root-sum-of-squares of its entries; (3) $A - A_k$ has the SVD $\sum_{i=k+1}^{r} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$, so $\|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2}$.

Now suppose $B$ has rank $\leq k$. Then $\ker(B)$ has dimension $\geq n - k$. The subspace $\text{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_{k+1}\}$ has dimension $k+1$. Since $(n - k) + (k + 1) = n + 1 > n$, these subspaces must intersect — there exists a unit vector $z \in \ker(B) \cap \text{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_{k+1}\}$.

Write $z = \sum_{i=1}^{k+1} \alpha_i v_i$ with $\sum \alpha_i^2 = 1$. Then $Bz = 0$, so:

$$\|A - B\|_F^2 \geq \|(A - B)z\|^2 = \|Az\|^2 = \left\|\sum_{i=1}^{k+1} \alpha_i \sigma_i u_i\right\|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{k+1} \alpha_i^2 \sigma_i^2 \geq \sigma_{k+1}^2$$

Since $\|A - A_k\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}$, this shows $A_k$ achieves the minimum in spectral norm. For the Frobenius norm bound, apply the same dimension argument iteratively to show that $\|A - B\|_F^2 \geq \sum_{i=k+1}^{r} \sigma_i^2$.
</TheoremBlock>

### Explained Variance

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={5} title="Explained variance ratio">
The fraction of total "energy" retained by the rank-$k$ approximation is:

$$\text{Explained variance ratio}(k) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i^2}$$

This ratio tells us how much of the matrix's structure we capture with $k$ components.
</TheoremBlock>

## Computing the SVD in Practice

### NumPy and SciPy

Two main functions for computing the SVD in Python:

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.sparse.linalg import svds

# Dense SVD (full or thin)
U, sigma, Vt = np.linalg.svd(A, full_matrices=False)  # thin SVD

# CAUTION: numpy returns Vt (V-transpose), NOT V.
# This is the single most common SVD bug in practice.
# To reconstruct: A ≈ U @ np.diag(sigma) @ Vt
# NOT: A ≈ U @ np.diag(sigma) @ V

# Sparse SVD (for large matrices, top k components only)
U_k, sigma_k, Vt_k = svds(A_sparse, k=50)
```

<TheoremBlock type="remark">
**The `Vt` pitfall.** `numpy.linalg.svd` returns $V^T$, not $V$. Nearly every SVD tutorial, homework solution, and production codebase has been bitten by this at least once. The variable name in numpy is literally `Vt` — use it. If you see code that does `U @ np.diag(sigma) @ V`, that's a bug.
</TheoremBlock>

### The Golub–Kahan Algorithm (Sketch)

The standard algorithm for computing the SVD in practice (used internally by LAPACK, which numpy calls) works in two phases:

1. **Bidiagonalization:** Reduce $A$ to upper bidiagonal form $B = U_1^T A V_1$ using Householder reflections. This costs $O(mn^2)$ for $m \geq n$.
2. **Diagonalization:** Apply the implicit QR algorithm to $B$ (iteratively chasing off-diagonal entries to zero). Each iteration costs $O(n)$, and convergence is typically cubic.

The total cost is $O(mn^2)$ for the thin SVD of an $m \times n$ matrix with $m \geq n$.

### Randomized SVD

For very large matrices where we only need the top $k$ singular values, the randomized SVD of Halko, Martinsson, and Tropp (2011) is dramatically faster:

```python
def randomized_svd(A, k, p=10):
    """
    Randomized SVD: compute rank-k approximation of A.

    Parameters:
        A: (m, n) matrix
        k: target rank
        p: oversampling parameter (default 10)

    Returns:
        U_k, sigma_k, Vt_k: truncated SVD components
    """
    m, n = A.shape
    l = k + p  # oversampled rank

    # Step 1: Random projection to find approximate range of A
    Omega = np.random.randn(n, l)       # random test matrix
    Y = A @ Omega                        # project A onto random subspace
    Q, _ = np.linalg.qr(Y)              # orthonormal basis for range(Y)

    # Step 2: Form the small matrix B = Q^T A (l × n)
    B = Q.T @ A

    # Step 3: Compute exact SVD of the small matrix B
    U_tilde, sigma, Vt = np.linalg.svd(B, full_matrices=False)

    # Step 4: Recover U = Q @ U_tilde
    U = Q @ U_tilde

    # Truncate to rank k
    return U[:, :k], sigma[:k], Vt[:k, :]
```

The key insight: $Y = A \Omega$ is an $m \times l$ matrix whose columns approximately span the column space of $A$. The QR factorization gives us an orthonormal basis $Q$, and then we only need to SVD the small $l \times n$ matrix $Q^T A$. Total cost: $O(mn \cdot l)$ instead of $O(mn^2)$.

## Application: Image Compression

A grayscale image is a matrix of pixel intensities. The SVD decomposes it into rank-1 "image layers," each capturing structure at a different scale. The leading singular values correspond to large-scale gradients and shapes; the trailing ones encode fine textures and noise.

The rank-$k$ truncated SVD stores only $k(m + n + 1)$ numbers instead of $mn$ — a compression ratio of $mn / k(m + n + 1)$. For a $512 \times 512$ image with $k = 50$, that's a 5× compression with the Eckart–Young guarantee of optimality.

Drag the rank slider to watch the image sharpen as you add more singular values:

<ImageCompressionDemo client:visible />

![Image compression: original and truncated SVD reconstructions at various ranks](/images/topics/svd/svd_image_compression.png)

```python
# SVD image compression
from PIL import Image
import numpy as np

img = np.array(Image.open('photo.png').convert('L'), dtype=float)
U, sigma, Vt = np.linalg.svd(img, full_matrices=False)

# Reconstruct at rank k
k = 50
img_k = U[:, :k] @ np.diag(sigma[:k]) @ Vt[:k, :]

# Statistics
storage_original = img.shape[0] * img.shape[1]
storage_compressed = k * (img.shape[0] + img.shape[1] + 1)
energy = np.sum(sigma[:k]**2) / np.sum(sigma**2)

print(f"Rank-{k}: {storage_compressed/storage_original:.1%} of original")
print(f"Energy retained: {energy:.1%}")
print(f"Frobenius error: {np.sqrt(np.sum(sigma[k:]**2)):.2f}")
```

## Application: Latent Semantic Analysis

In natural language processing, a **term-document matrix** $A$ has rows indexed by vocabulary terms and columns indexed by documents, with entries measuring word frequency (often TF-IDF weighted). This matrix is large, sparse, and noisy. The truncated SVD discovers **latent semantic dimensions** — hidden topic structure that explains word co-occurrence patterns.

The idea: if "car" and "automobile" never appear in the same document but both co-occur with "engine," "highway," and "driving," the SVD discovers that they belong to the same latent dimension. Documents that use different words but discuss the same topic will have similar representations in the SVD coordinate system.

The procedure is simple:

1. Build the term-document matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{|V| \times |D|}$ (often TF-IDF weighted).
2. Compute the rank-$k$ truncated SVD: $A_k = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^T$.
3. The rows of $\Sigma_k V_k^T$ give $k$-dimensional document embeddings.
4. The rows of $U_k \Sigma_k$ give $k$-dimensional term embeddings.
5. Cosine similarity in the reduced space measures semantic similarity.

```python
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
from scipy.sparse.linalg import svds

# Build TF-IDF matrix
corpus = [
    "the cat sat on the mat",
    "the dog chased the cat",
    "the cat and dog played",
    "linear algebra is beautiful",
    "matrices and vectors are fundamental",
    "eigenvalues describe matrix behavior",
]
vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer()
A = vectorizer.fit_transform(corpus)

# Truncated SVD for LSA
k = 2
U, sigma, Vt = svds(A.T, k=k)  # transpose: docs as rows

# svds returns singular values in ascending order — reverse to descending
idx = np.argsort(sigma)[::-1]
U, sigma, Vt = U[:, idx], sigma[idx], Vt[idx, :]

# Document embeddings in concept space
doc_embeddings = Vt.T * sigma  # (n_docs, k)

# Cosine similarity between documents
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity
sim = cosine_similarity(doc_embeddings)
print("Document similarity (in SVD concept space):")
print(sim.round(3))
```

This is **Latent Semantic Analysis** (Deerwester et al., 1990) — the original word embedding technique, and still one of the clearest examples of why the SVD matters for machine learning.

## The SVD–PCA Connection

PCA computes the directions of maximum variance in a centered data matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ (rows = observations). The standard approach is to eigendecompose the sample covariance matrix $\hat{\Sigma} = \frac{1}{n-1} X^T X$. But the SVD provides a numerically superior alternative.

Let $X = U \Sigma V^T$ be the thin SVD. Then:

$$\hat{\Sigma} = \frac{1}{n-1} X^T X = \frac{1}{n-1} V \Sigma^T U^T U \Sigma V^T = V \left(\frac{\Sigma^2}{n-1}\right) V^T$$

This is exactly the spectral decomposition of $\hat{\Sigma}$. We read off:

- **Principal components**: the columns of $V$ (right singular vectors of $X$).
- **Eigenvalues of $\hat{\Sigma}$**: $\lambda_i = \sigma_i^2 / (n-1)$.
- **Projected data**: $X V_k = U_k \Sigma_k$ (the principal component scores).

The SVD approach is numerically superior because it avoids forming $X^T X$ — which squares the condition number. If $\kappa(X) = 10^4$, then $\kappa(X^T X) = 10^8$, and eigendecomposition of $X^T X$ may lose 8 digits of precision. The SVD of $X$ itself keeps full precision.

```python
# PCA via SVD vs. covariance eigendecomposition
X = np.random.randn(100, 5)
X -= X.mean(axis=0)  # center the data

# Method 1: eigendecomposition of covariance matrix
Sigma_hat = (X.T @ X) / (X.shape[0] - 1)
eigenvalues, Q = np.linalg.eigh(Sigma_hat)
eigenvalues = eigenvalues[::-1]  # descending
Q = Q[:, ::-1]

# Method 2: SVD of data matrix
U, sigma, Vt = np.linalg.svd(X, full_matrices=False)
pca_eigenvalues = sigma**2 / (X.shape[0] - 1)

# They match
print(f"Eigenvalues (eigh):  {eigenvalues.round(6)}")
print(f"Eigenvalues (SVD):   {pca_eigenvalues.round(6)}")
print(f"Max difference:      {np.max(np.abs(eigenvalues - pca_eigenvalues)):.2e}")

# Principal components match (up to sign)
print(f"\nPC directions match: {np.allclose(np.abs(Q), np.abs(Vt.T))}")
```

## Connections & Further Reading

The SVD sits at the center of the Linear Algebra track, connecting backward to the Spectral Theorem and forward to PCA and tensor methods. It also has deep connections to the Topology & TDA track.

| **[The Spectral Theorem](/topics/spectral-theorem)** | The SVD proof relies on the Spectral Theorem applied to $A^T A$. For symmetric $A$, the SVD reduces to the spectral decomposition — singular values are absolute eigenvalues, and $U = V$ (up to sign). |
| **[PCA & Low-Rank Approximation](/topics/pca-low-rank)** | PCA is the SVD of the centered data matrix. Principal components are right singular vectors. The Eckart–Young theorem makes the truncated SVD the optimal low-rank projection. |
| **[Tensor Decompositions](/topics/tensor-decompositions)** | The CP decomposition generalizes the SVD outer product form $A = \sum \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$ to tensors: $\mathcal{T} = \sum \lambda_i \, a_i \otimes b_i \otimes c_i$. HOSVD extends the SVD to multilinear algebra. |
| **[Persistent Homology](/topics/persistent-homology)** | The boundary matrices $\partial_k$ in a simplicial complex have an SVD. The rank of $\partial_k$ determines Betti numbers, and the singular values quantify topological feature strength. |
